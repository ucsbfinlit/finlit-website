Dashboard Overview

This dashboard tracks the club's simulated portfolio in real time. Cost Basis reflects the original purchase price per share. Current Value is the mark-to-market valuation. Max Drift indicates the largest deviation from target allocation.

Portfolio Risk Metrics ?These numbers tell you about the risk profile of the portfolio as a whole. Risk is defined as volatility — how much your investment goes up and down. Higher risk can mean higher rewards, but also bigger losses. Understanding your risk helps you make better decisions.

Risk Metrics

Sharpe Ratio measures risk-adjusted return per unit of volatility. Values above 1.0 indicate favorable risk-adjusted performance. Portfolio Beta quantifies systematic risk relative to the S&P 500 (1.0 = market equivalent). Concentration measures single-position risk exposure.

Sector Diversification ?The stock market is divided into sectors: Technology, Industrials, Healthcare, Energy, Financials, and more. Each has a different risk/reward profile. Spreading your stock picks across multiple sectors protects you if one industry has a rough patch. This is diversification within your alpha picks.

Sector Allocation

Asset class diversification (the 60/30/10 model) manages macro-level risk. Sector diversification adds a second layer within each asset class. Concentrating alpha positions in a single sector exposes the portfolio to industry-specific drawdowns. Distributing holdings across multiple sectors reduces unsystematic risk.

Allocation ?How your portfolio splits across asset classes. A good portfolio mixes different types of investments — stocks/ETFs for growth, bonds for stability. Our target is 60% index, 30% alpha picks, 10% fixed income. This is asset allocation — the most important decision in portfolio construction.
Allocation vs Target ?Shows how far each category has drifted from our 60/30/10 target. The white marker is the target; the colored bar is actual. When drift gets too large, we rebalance.

Module 1: Investment Basics
Start here if you have never invested before.
Slides: Paste your Google Slides embed link here
This module covers what stocks, bonds, and ETFs are, how to build a portfolio, and how to open your first brokerage account.
What is a stock?▼
A stock is a small piece of ownership in a company. When you buy a share of Apple, you literally own a fraction of Apple. As a shareholder, you are entitled to a part of that company's profits. If the company does well, your shares go up in value. If it does poorly, they go down.
What is a bond?▼
A bond is a loan you make to somebody, usually the government or a big company. They pay you interest on that loan (called coupon payments, usually every 6 months), and at the end of the term they give you your money back. Bonds are lower risk than stocks. They preserve your wealth and give you steady, predictable income. Terms range from 1 to 30 years.
What is an ETF?▼
An ETF is a basket of investments bundled into one thing you can buy like a stock. That basket can hold stocks, bonds, bitcoin, gold, basically anything. The value of the ETF equals whatever is inside it. We love ETFs because they are accessible, have low fees, and give you easy diversification. Instead of buying 500 individual stocks, you buy one ETF like VOO and you own all of them.
What is a portfolio?▼
A portfolio is all your investments together. The most important thing about a portfolio is diversification: mix it up so that if one investment does poorly, others can balance it out. Before you invest, think about your income, your expenses, and how much you can realistically put aside after building an emergency fund.
How much money do you need to start?▼
As little as one cent. Most modern brokerages like Fidelity, Schwab, and Vanguard have no account minimums. You can buy fractional shares. The point is to start, even if it is small.
How to open an investment account▼
Choose a brokerage (Fidelity, Schwab, or Vanguard are all solid). Apply online with your SSN, an ID, and bank info. Deposit money and buy your first stock or ETF. A regular brokerage account has no limits but you pay taxes on gains. A Roth IRA grows tax-free but has contribution limits. A 401(k) is through your employer and may include matching. For most students, start with a brokerage account or a Roth IRA.
Module 2: Risk and Reward
Understanding why some investments go up more than others.
Slides: Paste your Google Slides embed link here
This module covers risk, reward, market sectors, value vs growth investing, and why patience is your biggest advantage.
What is risk?▼
Risk is volatility. It is how much your investment goes up and down. A stock that swings 5% in a day is riskier than one that barely moves. Risk is not bad or good. It is just something you need to be aware of.
Risk and reward are connected▼
When you take on more risk, you are compensated with potentially higher returns. Lower risk gives you lower but more predictable returns. That tradeoff is the most fundamental idea in investing. There is no free lunch.
Time reduces risk▼
Investing over a long period of time reduces your exposure to risk. Big ups and downs are okay when you are not selling anytime soon. As a college student, time is your biggest advantage. You have decades ahead, which means you can take on more risk now and ride out the bumps.
Market sectors▼
The stock market is divided into sectors: Technology, Healthcare, Financials, Energy, Industrials, Consumer Staples, Consumer Discretionary, Communication Services, Utilities, Real Estate, and Basic Materials. Each has a different risk/reward profile. Tech is higher risk, higher reward. Utilities are lower risk, lower reward. Diversifying across sectors means one bad industry will not tank your whole portfolio.
Value vs Growth▼
Value investing is about finding companies that are cheap relative to what they are worth. If a good company is trading at a low price, there is more room for it to go up. Growth investing is about betting on companies growing fast. They tend to be more expensive, but you can still profit if they keep growing. Smaller companies and growth stocks are generally riskier. Larger value companies are more stable.
Patience beats timing▼
Institutions are better than you at day trading. Trying to buy low and sell high based on short-term moves is a losing game. The stock market is extremely efficient and information is priced in almost instantly. The real way to make money is to hold. Prices fluctuate, but the market always trends upwards over long periods. Remove emotion from your decision making.
Module 3: Picking Stocks
How to evaluate a company before you buy it.
Slides: Paste your Google Slides embed link here
This module covers financial statements, profitability metrics, growth analysis, and valuation.
Financial position▼
Before you buy a stock, look at how the company is doing right now. What do they own? What do they owe? Check the balance sheet. A quick measure: Debt to Assets ratio (Total Liabilities / Total Assets). It tells you what percentage of their stuff is paid for with debt. Lower is generally better.
Profitability▼
Gross Margin is Revenue minus Direct Costs, divided by Revenue. It tells you how much money is coming in before expenses. Net Margin subtracts everything and tells you how much the company actually keeps. EPS (Earnings Per Share) is Total Earnings divided by Shares Outstanding. It tells you how much of the company's profit one share entitles you to.
Growth and dividends▼
Look at revenue growth over time. Is the company making more money each year or slowing down? Some companies also pay dividends, which is a portion of profits paid directly to shareholders. Dividend Yield (dividend per share / stock price) tells you what percentage return you get just from holding.
Valuation▼
How expensive is it to invest in this company? The P/E Ratio (Stock Price / Earnings Per Share) tells you. A lower P/E means you pay less for each dollar of earnings. Cheaper is generally better, but fast-growing companies often have high P/E ratios because investors expect earnings to catch up. Compare P/E within the same industry, not across different ones.
Other factors to consider▼
Numbers are not everything. Who is running the company? What is happening in the broader economy and in business cycles? Who are the competitors and are they doing it better? All of these factors influence whether a stock is a good investment beyond just the financial statements.

Recommended Resources
Books
The Intelligent Investor by Benjamin Graham
The foundational text on value investing. Covers margin of safety, Mr. Market, and long-term investment principles.
A Random Walk Down Wall Street by Burton Malkiel
The case for index investing and efficient market theory. Core reading for understanding our 60/30/10 allocation rationale.
The Psychology of Money by Morgan Housel
Behavioral finance and the role of psychology in investment decision-making. Accessible introduction to common cognitive biases.
One Up On Wall Street by Peter Lynch
Practical framework for identifying investment opportunities through everyday observation and fundamental analysis.

Why Your Support Matters
The Financial Literacy Club at UC Santa Barbara is one of the only organizations on campus dedicated entirely to investment education. We serve students across all majors, most of whom have never been exposed to personal finance or portfolio management before college. Our members learn to analyze securities, build diversified portfolios, and develop the financial decision-making skills they will use for the rest of their lives.

We operate entirely on volunteer effort and donated resources. Your contribution directly funds the tools and programming that make this possible: research subscriptions, educational materials, guest speaker honorariums, event production, and the technology infrastructure behind our portfolio simulation platform.
